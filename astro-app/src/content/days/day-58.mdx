context: "<p>A product vision statement is the most compressed form of product strategy. It tells the team where you’re going and why it matters. For AI products, vision statements are especially tricky because the technology is evolving so fast that visions either become obsolete or so vague they mean nothing. Today you learn how to write an AI product vision that is aspirational but not vague, time-bounded but not timid, and product-grounded but not feature-limited.</p>\n  <p><strong>Vision failure modes to avoid.</strong> Three common failure modes kill AI product visions: (1) <strong>The capability claim</strong> — “We will build the most advanced AI assistant.” This isn’t a vision; it’s a capability claim that every AI company makes. It tells the team nothing about who you serve, what problem you solve, or why it matters. (2) <strong>The platform for all needs</strong> — “We will be the AI platform that serves every user and every use case.” This is a strategy to serve no one well. Great products are opinionated about who they serve and how. (3) <strong>“Democratizing AI”</strong> — the most overused phrase in AI product marketing. It sounds inspiring but means nothing concrete. Democratizing for whom? To do what? By when? If your vision could appear on any AI company’s website, it’s not specific enough to guide decisions.</p>\n  <p><strong>Good vision examples.</strong> Study these as models of effective AI product vision: (1) <strong>GitHub Copilot</strong> — a vision centered on making every developer more productive by handling routine coding tasks, so developers can focus on creative problem-solving. It’s specific (developers), bounded (coding tasks), and aspirational (more productive). (2) <strong>Harvey AI</strong> — a vision of transforming legal work by making AI the associate that handles research, drafting, and analysis, so lawyers can focus on judgment and client relationships. It’s specific (legal), bounded (associate-level work), and grounded (research/drafting/analysis). (3) <strong>Claude as a “brilliant friend”</strong> — Anthropic’s vision articulated as an AI that gives the quality of advice you’d get from a brilliant friend who happens to have the knowledge of a doctor, lawyer, and financial advisor. It’s aspirational (brilliant friend), grounded (advice quality), and relatable (everyone understands what a helpful friend means).</p>\n  <p><strong>The three-criteria test.</strong> Every AI product vision should pass three tests: (1) <strong>Aspirational, not vague</strong> — does it describe a future state that’s genuinely better? Can two people read it and agree on what success looks like? “More productive developers” passes. “Transforming the world with AI” fails. (2) <strong>Time-bounded</strong> — is there an implicit or explicit timeframe? A 3-year vision differs from a 10-year vision. Frontier AI moves so fast that 2–3 year vision horizons are appropriate. Beyond that, you’re writing science fiction, not product strategy. (3) <strong>Product-grounded</strong> — can you trace the vision back to specific product decisions? If the vision says “every developer more productive,” you can evaluate every feature against that: does this feature make a developer more productive? If a vision can’t be used to make feature prioritization decisions, it’s decoration, not strategy.</p>\n  <p><strong>Delivering your vision aloud.</strong> A vision that only works on paper isn’t effective. Product leaders deliver visions verbally — in team meetings, board presentations, candidate pitches, and customer conversations. The verbal version must be: (1) Under 60 seconds. If you can’t say it in a minute, it’s too complex. (2) Jargon-free. If a non-technical person can’t understand it, simplify. (3) Emotionally resonant. The best visions make people feel something — excitement about the future state, urgency about the problem, connection to the mission. Practice delivering your vision aloud until it sounds natural, not rehearsed. Record yourself. Listen. Revise.</p>"
